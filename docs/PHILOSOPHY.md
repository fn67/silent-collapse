# PHILOSOPHY.md — The Why Behind Every Decision

## Read This Before Making Any Judgment Call

When you face a design or code decision not explicitly covered by other docs,
come back to this file. Every ambiguous choice should be resolved by asking:
does this serve the core metaphor?

---

## The Core Metaphor

```
Black background  = potential. The universe before anything happens.
White particles   = life, activity, people showing up.
Red overlay       = systemic decay. Creeping. Slow. Inevitable if unchecked.
Silence / inaction = the actual risk. Not malice. Just... nothing.
Action            = participation. Not heroism. Just not looking away.
Collapse          = the accumulated weight of many small silences.
Recovery          = the hard work of confronting every silence you chose.
Restoration       = what becomes possible when accountability is chosen.
```

This is not a story about villains.
It is a story about ordinary people making ordinary choices to do nothing.
And about what it means to choose differently — even after the damage is done.

---

## Why There Is Now a Recovery Mechanic

In the original design there was no recovery. That was philosophically honest.

The recovery mechanic was added — but it does not contradict the original philosophy.
Here is why:

Recovery is not easy. It is not a reset button.
In recovery phase, the user must revisit every single moment they chose silence.
They must confront each one individually. They must act on it deliberately.
Only then does the universe begin to heal.

And even then — the red overlay only fully clears when every missed moment is addressed.
Not most of them. All of them.

This is the emotional truth of real accountability:
you don't get to selectively acknowledge your silences.
You have to go back and face them one by one.

Recovery is harder than not collapsing in the first place.
That is the point.

---

## Why Red Only Reduces in Recovery Phase

During normal play, taking action does not reduce existing decay.
The damage of past silences is already done.

This rule stays even in recovery phase logic:
red only reduces when the user is actively confronting a silence they already chose.
It does not reduce from general good behavior.

This distinction matters. It says:
good actions going forward do not automatically fix the past.
Only direct confrontation of past failures does.

---

## Why Recovery Can Repeat

The cycle of collapse → recovery can happen indefinitely.
This is intentional.

Because in real organizations, decay is not a one-time event.
Teams can rebuild culture, then let it decay again, then rebuild again.
The experience mirrors that reality.

There is no penalty for collapsing multiple times.
There is no reward for never collapsing.
There is only: did you choose to act when it mattered?

---

## Why the Pacing Is Slow

Decay is slow. Collapse is slow. Recovery is slow.
If this experience moved fast, it would feel like a game.
Games are escapist. This is not.

Every delay, every fade, every hold — it gives the user time to sit with the weight.
Fast = entertainment. Slow = meaning.

Never reduce the fade timings to speed things up.
If something feels too slow, that feeling is the point.

---

## Why Each Scenario Has a Pillar

The four pillars — Leadership, Innovation, Credibility, Togetherness — are not labels.
They are the architecture of a healthy universe.

Mapping each scenario to a pillar says:
every small silence damages something specific.
Ignoring a junior member's mentorship request damages Leadership.
Dismissing an idea without discussion damages Innovation.
Misattributing credit damages Credibility.
Leaving a conflict unaddressed damages Togetherness.

The pillar logo appearing with each scenario is a quiet reminder:
this is not just an abstract failing. It has a name. It belongs somewhere.

The logos should feel like a quiet presence, not a label.
A gentle pulse. A soft glow. Not a badge.

---

## Why There Is No Score

Scores imply optimization. Optimization implies detachment.
If you're playing for a score, you're not feeling the scenarios.

There is no leaderboard, no points, no stars.
There is only: did the universe survive, or did it collapse — and what did you do about it?

---

## Why the Scenarios Are About Ordinary Situations

None of the scenarios involve fraud, harassment, or dramatic moral failures.
They are all small, ordinary, "it's fine, someone else will handle it" moments.

Because that is where organizations actually decay.
Not in dramatic events — in the accumulation of reasonable-sounding inactions.

If you add new scenarios, they must stay in this register.
Small. Everyday. Easy to justify not acting on.

---

## The Tone of the Writing

All text — scenarios, phrases, end screens — follows one rule:

**Quiet authority. No drama.**

Not: "The team is FALLING APART because nobody cared!"
But: "A conflict between two members is quietly ignored."

Not: "YOU FAILED. The universe is DEAD."
But: "Universes don't collapse loudly. They fade quietly."

Every word should feel like it was chosen carefully.
Every sentence should be short.
No exclamation marks. Ever.

---

## The User's Emotional Journey

What we want the user to feel, in order:

1. **Curiosity** — What is this? It feels different.
2. **Weight** — These scenarios feel real. I recognize these.
3. **Discomfort** — The red is growing. I feel responsible.
4. **Collapse** — I let it happen.
5. **Reckoning** — I have to go back and face every moment I avoided.
6. **Accountability** — This is harder than I thought.
7. **Restoration** — The universe can be saved. But only through deliberate action.
8. **Intention** — I want to act differently. Here, and maybe elsewhere.

---

## Final Principle

> This is not a game about winning.
> It is an experience about responsibility.
> No explosions. No dramatic failures.
> Just gradual fade — and the choice to rebuild.

When in doubt, return to this line.
